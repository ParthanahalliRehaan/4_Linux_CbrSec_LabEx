## 📖 What is a Dictionary Attack?
- A **dictionary attack** is a type of password-cracking technique.  
- Instead of trying every possible combination of characters (like brute-force), attackers use a **precompiled list of common or likely passwords** — called a *dictionary*.  
- These dictionaries often include:
  - Default passwords (e.g., *admin*, *1234*).  
  - Common weak passwords (e.g., *password123*, *qwerty*, *letmein*).  
  - Variations of words with numbers/symbols (e.g., *summer2024!*, *welcome@123*).  
  - Leaked passwords from past breaches.

---

## ⚡ How It Differs from Brute-Force
| **Brute-Force Attack** | **Dictionary Attack** |
|-------------------------|------------------------|
| Tries *every possible combination* of characters until success. | Tries *likely passwords* from a curated list. |
| Very slow if password is long/complex. | Much faster because it skips unlikely guesses. |
| Guaranteed to succeed eventually (if unlimited attempts allowed). | Success depends on whether the password is in the dictionary. |

---

## 🛠️ Why Attackers Use It
- **Efficiency**: Most users pick predictable passwords, so attackers don’t need to test random strings.  
- **Real-world data**: Dictionaries are often built from leaked password databases, making them highly effective.  
- **Automation**: Tools can run through millions of dictionary entries in seconds.  

---

## 🔐 Defense Against Dictionary Attacks
1. **Strong, unique passwords**  
   - Use long phrases with mixed characters (letters, numbers, symbols).  
   - Avoid common words or predictable patterns.  
2. **Account protections**  
   - Lockouts after repeated failures.  
   - CAPTCHAs to block automated scripts.  
   - Rate limiting to slow down attempts.  
3. **Multi-factor authentication (MFA)**  
   - Even if a password is guessed, attackers need a second factor (like a phone code).  
4. **Password managers**  
   - Generate and store complex, random passwords that aren’t in any dictionary.  

---

## 🧩 Analogy
Imagine a thief trying to open a lock:
- **Brute-force**: They try *every possible key shape* until one works.  
- **Dictionary attack**: They bring a bag of *keys that people commonly use* — default keys, popular shapes, or ones stolen from other houses. It’s faster and more likely to succeed if the lock owner used a common key.

---

## 📌 Key Takeaway
A dictionary attack is essentially a **smarter brute-force**: it leverages human predictability in password choices. That’s why cybersecurity emphasizes **complex, unique passwords** and **layered defenses** — because attackers almost always start with dictionary lists before moving to full brute-force.
